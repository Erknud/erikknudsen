abstract: This study examines affective polarization in Norway compared to the United States, extending the concept of affective polarization to multiparty systems by distinguishing between polarization towards voters and parties. Drawing on original survey data from Norway and the US, the article finds that affective polarization is substantially lower in Norway than in the US, and that attitudes towards voters and parties are empirically distinct. The findings have implications for how we understand partisan identity and democratic functioning across different party systems.
